Research Focus

Our laboratory employs a multidisciplinary approach combining molecular biology, biochemistry, and bioinformatics.

1. tsRNA Biology in Health and Disease

Transfer RNA-derived small RNAs (tsRNAs) were long considered random degradation products. However, recent evidence suggests they are functional, regulated non-coding RNAs that play critical roles in gene expression.

Our lab investigates how tsRNA biogenesis is triggered by specific cellular stressors. Unlike microRNAs, tsRNAs can inhibit translation by displacing the initiation complex. We are mapping the "tsRNAome" in various cell lines to identify specific signatures associated with disease states.

2. Oxidative Stress and Nrf2 Signaling

The Nrf2 (nuclear factor erythroid 2-related factor 2) pathway is the cell's primary defense mechanism against oxidative stress. Dysregulation of this pathway is a hallmark of both neurodegenerative diseases and cancer.

We are exploring the crosstalk between non-coding RNAs and Nrf2. Specifically, we aim to understand how certain RNA species can sequester Nrf2 in the cytoplasm or promote its translocation to the nucleus, thereby altering the cell's antioxidant response.

3. Mechanisms of Therapy Resistance

Glioblastoma is the most aggressive form of brain cancer. Standard of care involves the alkylating agent Temozolomide (TMZ), but resistance develops rapidly.

Our hypothesis is that stress-induced RNAs and elevated Nrf2 activity confer a survival advantage to cancer cells against TMZ. By defining these molecular mechanisms, we hope to identify novel targets that can re-sensitize resistant tumors to chemotherapy.
